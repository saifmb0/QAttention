\begin{abstract}
Speculative decoding accelerates large-language-model inference by verifying
trees of draft tokens in a single target-model forward pass. State-of-the-art
tree optimisers --- EAGLE-3 and Sequoia --- consistently converge to small,
shallow trees. We argue this is not because deeper or wider trees are
algorithmically inferior, but because the attention kernels on which these
systems rely (FlashInfer, DeFT) scale quadratically in tree size, making the
larger trees that would yield more accepted tokens prohibitively slow.
The optimiser is therefore trapped in a kernel-induced local minimum.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Speculative decoding~\cite{leviathan2023fast,chen2023accelerating} reduces
the latency of autoregressive generation by drafting multiple candidate
tokens with a small model and \emph{verifying} them in parallel with the
large target model. Modern systems organise the candidates into a
\emph{tree}: each path from the root corresponds to one speculative
continuation, and a tree of $N$ nodes lets the verifier consider $N$
candidate continuations in a single forward pass. Tree-based variants such
as EAGLE-2/EAGLE-3~\cite{li2024eagle,li2024eagle2,li2025eagle3} and
Sequoia~\cite{chen2024sequoia} are now the de facto framework for
speculative decoding in production stacks.

\paragraph{The local-minimum problem.}
Empirically, tree optimisers --- whether learned (EAGLE-2's expansion
policy) or analytic (Sequoia's dynamic-programming size selection) ---
converge to relatively small, shallow trees ($N \!\lesssim\! 100$,
$d \!\le\! 6$). The standard explanation is that acceptance rates plateau
with tree size; deeper trees offer diminishing returns. We argue this
explanation is incomplete. The kernels that implement tree-attention in
production --- FlashInfer~\cite{ye2025flashinfer} and DeFT~\cite{yao2024deft}
--- still process $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ work per sequence: they iterate over
all KV blocks and apply a tree mask. Larger trees therefore inflate
attention latency \emph{quadratically}, while the marginal token gain is
sub-linear. The optimiser is steered away from large trees not because
they are algorithmically poor, but because the kernel makes them expensive.

In a BFS-ordered $b$-ary tree, however, query $q$ can attend to at most
$d{+}1$ positions: the chain of ancestors from $q$ up to the root. The
\emph{useful} attention work is $\mathcal{O}(N\!\cdot\!d)$, not
$\mathcal{O}(N^2)$. FlashInfer's iterate-and-mask formulation discards this
sparsity. Once the kernel is rewritten to walk the ancestor chain
explicitly, the quadratic wall vanishes.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item An ancestor-sparse Flash Attention-2 kernel for tree-structured
      speculative decoding implemented in Triton, with
      $\mathcal{O}(N\!\cdot\!d)$ FMAs and HBM traffic
      (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).
\item A grid-search evaluation across $(b, d, B, L)$ showing consistent
      wins in the deep/wide regime, with up to $7.75\times$ over
      FlashInfer at $b{=}28,\,d{=}28$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:eval-micro}).
\item An Amdahl analysis on EAGLE-3 demonstrating that, once attention is
      no longer the bottleneck, MLP forward dominates and the verify
      fraction drops from $0.73$ to $0.47$ as the tree deepens
      (Sec.~\ref{sec:eval-amdahl}).
\item End-to-end results: up to \textbf{$1.40\times$} on
      Sequoia, and a quantitative explanation for why the EAGLE-3 gain is
      smaller --- acceptance-rate decay (Sec.~\ref{sec:eval-e2e}). We name
      this remaining ceiling \emph{Wall 2} and leave it to future work.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Tree verification.}
Given a target model and a draft tree of $N$ nodes in BFS order, the
verifier runs one forward pass over all $N$ tokens. The attention mask is
\emph{ancestor-causal}: query at position $i$ may attend to position $j$
iff $j$ is an ancestor of $i$ in the tree, or $j {=} i$. For a complete
$b$-ary tree of depth $d$, $\text{parent}(k) = \lfloor (k{-}1)/b \rfloor$
for $k > 0$, and $N = (b^{d+1}{-}1)/(b{-}1)$.

\paragraph{Why dense / padded attention is wasteful.}
Naive verification pads each tree to the largest $N$ in the batch and
applies a dense attention with a tree mask. The wasted-FLOPs ratio grows
sharply with $b$ and $d$: at $b{=}4,\,d{=}5$, $55.6\%$ of all attention
FLOPs are masked away.\footnote{Analytic padding-waste model from the
accompanying source release.}
More importantly, even after dense padding is eliminated, kernels that
loop over KV blocks and apply the mask still do $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ work:
a query at depth $d$ attends to only $d{+}1$ keys, but the kernel reads
all $N$.

\paragraph{Existing kernels and what we exclude.}
\textbf{FlashInfer}~\cite{ye2025flashinfer} provides a generalised
tree-attention kernel that handles arbitrary tree shapes; it is the de
facto deployment standard, and is our primary baseline.
\textbf{DeFT}~\cite{yao2024deft} requires \emph{strictly balanced} trees
and is incompatible with the pruned, irregular trees produced by EAGLE-2/3
expansion policies. On the balanced trees it does support, our
measurements (and prior reports) show it is roughly $2\times$ slower than
FlashInfer with poor scaling. We therefore exclude DeFT as a primary
baseline. We likewise exclude \textbf{w4a16} quantised pipelines: they
require specialised inference stacks not standard in production
deployments and are orthogonal to the attention-kernel question.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

We replace the KV-block loop of a standard Flash Attention-2
kernel~\cite{dao2023flashattention2} with an \emph{ancestor-walk} loop of
exactly $d_{\max}{+}1$ iterations. Each iteration performs a
\emph{scattered gather}: for every query row $q$ in a $\text{BLOCK\_M}$
tile, we load $K[\text{parent}^s(q)]$ and $V[\text{parent}^s(q)]$ at
ancestor depth $s$. The Flash Attention-2 online softmax update applies
\emph{verbatim} --- it is agnostic to whether the per-step contribution is
a $\text{BLOCK\_N}$-block of scores or a single ancestor score. A
duplicate guard, $\text{prev}\!\neq\!\text{cur}$, makes iterations after
the root has been reached into no-ops, which lets shallow queries share
the same kernel without branching.

\paragraph{Parent computation on Turing.}
A subtle PTX issue arises with non-power-of-two $b$. The natural
expression \verb|cur = (cur - 1) // b| compiles to an unsigned
multiply-high; when $\text{cur}{=}0$, the operand $-1$ becomes
$0\text{x}\text{FFFFFFFF}$ and produces a spurious ancestor index that
poisons the mask. We clamp \emph{before} dividing:
\verb|cur = max(cur - 1, 0) // b|, which guarantees a non-negative
dividend.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Ancestor-sparse Flash Attention-2 inner loop (single Q-tile, single head).}
\label{alg:kernel}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Q, K, V; sequence offsets, branching factor $b$, max depth $D$
\State $\text{acc} \gets 0;\;\; m_i \gets -\infty;\;\; l_i \gets 0$
\State $\text{cur} \gets q\_\text{positions};\;\; \text{prev} \gets -1$
\For{$s = 0$ \textbf{to} $D$}                            \Comment{ancestor walk}
  \State $\text{is\_new} \gets (\text{cur} \neq \text{prev}) \wedge \text{valid}$
  \State $k_a \gets \mathrm{load}(K[\text{seq\_start} + \text{cur}])$
  \State $v_a \gets \mathrm{load}(V[\text{seq\_start} + \text{cur}])$
  \State $s \gets \langle q, k_a\rangle \cdot \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{D_h}}\;\;\text{(masked $-\infty$ where not is\_new)}$
  \State $m_{\text{new}} \gets \max(m_i, s);\;\; \alpha \gets e^{m_i - m_{\text{new}}};\;\; p \gets e^{s - m_{\text{new}}}$
  \State $\text{acc} \gets \alpha \cdot \text{acc} + p \cdot v_a;\;\; l_i \gets \alpha\cdot l_i + p;\;\; m_i \gets m_{\text{new}}$
  \State $\text{prev} \gets \text{cur};\;\; \text{cur} \gets \max(\text{cur}-1,0)\,\,/\!/\,\,b$
\EndFor
\State \Return $\text{acc} / l_i$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Complexity.}
For a complete $b$-ary tree of depth $d$ with head dimension $D_h$ and $H$
heads per token, the ancestor-sparse kernel performs
$\mathcal{O}(B\!\cdot\!N\!\cdot\!(d{+}1)\!\cdot\!D_h\!\cdot\!H)$ FMAs and
streams $\mathcal{O}(B\!\cdot\!N\!\cdot\!(d{+}1)\!\cdot\!D_h\!\cdot\!H)$
bytes of K/V, versus $\mathcal{O}(B\!\cdot\!N^2\!\cdot\!D_h\!\cdot\!H)$ for
dense or block-masked tree attention. At $B{=}4,\,b{=}4,\,d{=}5,\,N{=}1365,\,
D_h{=}64,\,H{=}8,\,\text{BLOCK\_M}{=}64$, this yields $470\times$ fewer
FMAs and $3.6\times$ less HBM traffic per Q-tile relative to the dense
variant, with all unique K/V positions ($\approx 341$\,KB) fitting
comfortably in the T4 L2 (3.8\,MB) so cross-tile reuse is essentially
free.

\paragraph{Implementation notes.}
The kernel uses \texttt{num\_stages=1}: the load address at step $s{+}1$
is data-dependent on the value of \texttt{cur} computed at step $s$, so
the Triton software pipeliner cannot overlap iterations. We autotune on
$(\textsc{HeadDim}, \textsc{BranchingFactor}, \textsc{MaxDepth})$ ---
the three \texttt{constexpr} arguments that determine the compiled PTX.
On SM75 (Turing), \texttt{tl.dot} requires matching dtypes, so we keep
fp16 loads and pass \texttt{out\_dtype=tl.float32}. We compute
\texttt{max\_seqlen} on the CPU side from the cumulative-sequence-lengths
tensor, which is already CPU-resident, eliminating a per-call blocking
GPU\,$\to$\,CPU sync that cost $20$--$50\,\mu s$ in the original
implementation.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:eval-setup}

All experiments run on an NVIDIA Tesla T4 (SM75, 16\,GB HBM, $\approx$300
GB/s, 65 fp16 TFLOPS, 3.8\,MB L2). End-to-end runs use
Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct with the EAGLE3-LLaMA3.1-8B
draft~\cite{li2025eagle3}, fp16 weights and activations. Each
micro-benchmark measurement is the median of 50 CUDA-event-timed calls
after 10 warmup iterations; both kernels are timed by the same method.
We use FlashInfer~\cite{ye2025flashinfer} as the baseline kernel
throughout. As discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:background}, DeFT and w4a16 are
excluded for incompatibility / pipeline reasons rather than performance
reasons.

Figs.~\ref{fig:micro-fixed-b}--\ref{fig:micro-fixed-d} sweep the
verification-attention kernel across branching factor and depth at zero
prefix length. Three observations stand out. First, in the small-tree
regime ($d{\le}5,\, b{\le}4$) the two kernels track each other within
roughly $0.97\times$, dominated on both sides by Triton/cuDNN dispatch
floors. Second, FlashInfer's curves bend up super-linearly with $b$ and
$d$ --- the quadratic in $N$ asserts itself. Third, our curves remain
near-flat in the depth dimension: each query touches only $d{+}1$
ancestors, so adding nodes that are siblings (rather than deeper paths)
does not increase per-query work. By $(b,d){=}(28,28)$,
$N{=}681$, our kernel runs at $0.028$\,ms versus FlashInfer's
$0.216$\,ms --- a $7.75\times$ gap.

Fig.~\ref{fig:micro-sweep-n} sweeps total tree size with $b{=}4$ across
prefix lengths $L \in \{0, 4096, 16384, 32768\}$. Latency scales
approximately linearly in $N$ at each $L$, confirming the
$\mathcal{O}(N\!\cdot\!d)$ analysis end-to-end on hardware.

\subsection{Amdahl analysis: attention is no longer the bottleneck}
\label{sec:eval-amdahl}

Fig.~\ref{fig:amdahl} aggregates EAGLE-3 runs at fixed total-token budget
$tt{=}123$ across draft-tree depths. The left axis tracks accepted
tokens per step; the right axis tracks the fraction of step time spent in
the verifier (attention + MLP forward).

Two effects coexist. The verify fraction decreases from $0.73$ at $d{=}6$
to $0.47$ at $d{=}24$: with our kernel handling attention, the verifier
is now disproportionately MLP-bound, and the rest of the step (drafter
forward + tree management + sampling) becomes a larger relative slice.
Meanwhile, accepted tokens per step rises from $5.02$ to only $5.40$ ---
a $\approx 7\%$ improvement for a $4\times$ deeper tree. We call this
\emph{acceptance-rate decay}: each additional level adds candidates that
are increasingly unlikely to be accepted given the conditioning prefix.
Together, these two effects describe \emph{Wall 2}: the new ceiling on
end-to-end speedup once attention overhead is gone.

\subsection{End-to-end speedups}
\label{sec:eval-e2e}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.49\linewidth}\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig5_e2e_sequoia}
\caption{Sequoia E2E throughput speedup vs.\ tree size. Sequoia uses
fixed balanced trees and exploits larger sizes directly; we reach
$1.40\times$ at $\text{tree size}{=}1024$.}
\label{fig:e2e-sequoia}
\end{minipage}\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.49\linewidth}\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig6_e2e_eagle}
\caption{EAGLE-3 E2E throughput speedup vs.\ total tokens per tree. The

\label{fig:e2e-eagle}
\end{minipage}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Sequoia (Fig.~\ref{fig:e2e-sequoia}).}
Sequoia's tree-size sweep is the cleanest test of the two-wall thesis: it
fixes a \emph{balanced} tree shape and varies only the number of nodes,
so larger trees are exploited as advertised. Throughput is roughly flat
to size 256 (where attention is small relative to MLP), and our kernel
pulls away as the tree grows: $1.08\times$ at 512, $1.40\times$ at 1024.
The crossover is exactly where FlashInfer's quadratic term starts to
dominate the step time.

\paragraph{EAGLE-3 (Fig.~\ref{fig:e2e-eagle}).}
EAGLE-3's pruned trees with $tt \le 500$ live in the regime where our
micro-benchmark already showed near-parity. We see at most
$\approx 1.01\times$ end-to-end. This is consistent with our Amdahl
analysis: with EAGLE-3's pruning, attention is already a small fraction
of step time, and acceptance-rate decay caps the benefit of expanding
the tree further. The smaller end-to-end gain is therefore evidence for
\emph{Wall 2}, not against the kernel.

\paragraph{Reading the two figures together.}
The same kernel produces a $1.40\times$ E2E speedup on Sequoia and a
$1.01\times$ E2E speedup on EAGLE-3. The kernel is constant; the system
context is not. Where the optimiser is allowed (Sequoia) or forced
(balanced trees) into the regime our kernel targets, the wins translate
directly. Where the optimiser has already pruned to small trees (EAGLE-3),
removing the attention cost only matters once the optimiser is freed to
explore larger trees --- which is itself blocked by acceptance-rate decay.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Speculative decoding.}
Speculative sampling~\cite{leviathan2023fast,chen2023accelerating} and
its tree-based descendants~\cite{cai2024medusa,li2024eagle,li2024eagle2,li2025eagle3,
chen2024sequoia,miao2024specinfer,spector2023accelerating} have become
a standard inference acceleration technique. Optimisers in this line
universally target tree shape (depth, breadth, expansion policy)
\emph{given} a fixed attention kernel. Our work is complementary:
we change the kernel, which expands the design space the optimiser
operates in.

\paragraph{Attention kernels.}
Flash Attention~\cite{dao2022flashattention,dao2023flashattention2}
established the IO-aware online-softmax recipe we extend.
FlashInfer~\cite{ye2025flashinfer} provides production-quality
batched attention kernels including a generalised tree variant; it is
our baseline. DeFT~\cite{yao2024deft} targets balanced trees and is
incompatible with the irregular trees produced by EAGLE-style
optimisers, as discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:background}. Block-sparse and
mask-aware kernels~\cite{xformers2022,gao2024seerattention} share the
spirit of exploiting structured sparsity, but operate on
$\mathcal{O}(\text{block})$ granularity rather than the
$\mathcal{O}(d)$ ancestor structure specific to speculation trees.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented an ancestor-sparse Flash Attention kernel for
speculative-decoding tree verification with $\mathcal{O}(N\!\cdot\!d)$
work and HBM traffic, replacing the $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ inner loop of
production tree-attention kernels. Across micro-benchmarks we reach up
to $7.75\times$ over FlashInfer in the deep/wide regime; end-to-end we
deliver $1.40\times$ on Sequoia. \emph{Wall 1} --- attention overhead ---
is broken. The new ceiling, \emph{Wall 2} --- the joint product of
acceptance-rate decay and MLP-forward dominance --- is orthogonal to
attention. Productive directions for Wall 2 include drafter-side
techniques that improve acceptance per node and verifier-side techniques
that reduce MLP cost (e.g., conditional computation, MLP-quantisation
pipelines that we deliberately scoped out of this work). Our kernel makes
those investigations meaningful by ensuring attention will not silently
re-emerge as the bottleneck once the rest of the pipeline is improved.
