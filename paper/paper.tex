% ABSTRACT
% =====================================================================
\begin{abstract}
Tree-structured speculative decoding---used by
EAGLE-2~\cite{li2024eagle2}, EAGLE-3~\cite{li2025eagle3},
Medusa~\cite{cai2024medusa}, and Sequoia~\cite{chen2024sequoia}---verifies
hundreds to thousands of draft tokens per step.
During verification, each token attends only to its
$\depth{+}1$ ancestors, yet every production attention kernel
(FlashAttention-2~\cite{dao2023flashattention2},
FlashInfer~\cite{ye2025flashinfer}, DeFT~\cite{yao2024deft})
performs $\bigO{\numtokens^2}$ work, blind to this sparsity.
We present \textbf{\method{}}, a Triton kernel that
exploits BFS tree structure to reduce per-head complexity from
$\bigO{\numtokens^2}$ to $\bigO{\numtokens \cdot \depth}$.
The kernel replaces the KV-block loop of FlashAttention-2
with an ancestor walk of $\depth{+}1$ scattered gathers per Q-tile.
On an NVIDIA H100 SXM (Hopper, SM~9.0), \method{} is benchmarked
against the three strongest tree-attention baselines:
FlashAttention-2 (via PyTorch SDPA flash backend),
FlashInfer, and DeFT.
\method{} achieves \textbf{up to 49$\boldsymbol{\times}$} speedup
over FlashAttention-2,
\textbf{101$\boldsymbol{\times}$} over FlashInfer, and
\textbf{109$\boldsymbol{\times}$} over DeFT
at $(\branching{=}4, \depth{=}6, \batchsize{=}128)$.
At practical EAGLE-class tree sizes
$(\branching{=}3, \depth{=}7, \batchsize{=}8)$,
speedups are \textbf{11.9$\boldsymbol{\times}$},
\textbf{22.3$\boldsymbol{\times}$}, and
\textbf{45.6$\boldsymbol{\times}$} respectively.
At small tree sizes ($\numtokens < 500$), dense kernels remain faster
due to launch overhead; \method{}'s advantage grows monotonically
with tree depth and batch size.
We provide autotuned configurations for Turing~SM75 and Hopper~SM90,
demonstrate numerical correctness to $<10^{-3}$ max absolute error,
and characterize the quadratic padding waste dense kernels incur on
tree-shaped batches.
\textbf{Limitations.}\
The kernel handles \emph{intra-tree} attention only (not the context-prefix
KV cache), uses $\headdim{=}64$ (not the 128 common in LLaMA),
and benchmarks use synthetic weights.
All code, data, and reproduction scripts are open-sourced.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

Autoregressive decoding in large language models (LLMs) is
fundamentally memory-bound: each token generation requires a full
forward pass whose arithmetic intensity is limited by the
key--value (KV) cache read \cite{leviathan2023fast, chen2023accelerating}.
Speculative decoding addresses this by generating candidate tokens
cheaply with a \emph{draft model} and verifying them in parallel
with the \emph{target model} \cite{leviathan2023fast, chen2023accelerating}.

Recent methods—EAGLE-2 \cite{li2024eagle2}, EAGLE-3 \cite{li2025eagle3},
Medusa \cite{cai2024medusa}, Sequoia \cite{chen2024sequoia}, and
SpecInfer \cite{miao2024specinfer}—extend the draft from a single
chain to a \emph{tree} of candidate tokens, reaching
3--6$\times$ end-to-end speedup.
EAGLE-3, the current state of the art, achieves up to 6.5$\times$
speedup on LLaMA-3.1-Instruct-8B by fusing draft-head training into
the target model's pre-training objective~\cite{li2025eagle3}.
In a complete $\branching$-ary tree of depth $\depth$, the total
token count is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tree-size}
  \numtokens = \frac{\branching^{\depth+1} - 1}{\branching - 1},
  \quad \branching > 1.
\end{equation}
During verification, each draft token~$i$ in the tree may attend only
to its ancestors—the $\depth_i + 1$ nodes on the unique root-to-$i$
path—rather than to all $\numtokens$ tokens.  The resulting attention
mask is therefore \emph{extremely sparse}: for $\branching = 4$,
$\depth = 5$, only 6 of 1{,}365 positions are attended, representing
$0.44\%$ utilization of the full mask.

Despite this sparsity, all production-ready attention kernels
(FlashAttention-2 \cite{dao2023flashattention2}, SDPA backends,
FlashInfer \cite{ye2025flashinfer}) iterate over \emph{all} KV blocks
and rely on causal or boolean masks to zero out irrelevant positions,
performing $\bigO{\numtokens^2}$ work per head.
The closest prior work is DeFT \cite{yao2024deft} (ICLR~2025), which
groups queries by shared KV prefixes and achieves 3.59$\times$
attention speedup over FlashAttention-2 on A100.
DeFT's approach is \emph{KV-guided}: it partitions nodes into
subtrees and flattens depth-first for coalesced access.
Our approach is \emph{Q-guided}: each query independently walks its
$\depth{+}1$ ancestors using the closed-form BFS parent formula,
requiring no auxiliary data structures, no depth-first reordering,
and no bitmask storage.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item We present \textbf{\method{}}, an ancestor-sparse Flash
    Attention variant implemented as a Triton JIT kernel.
    It replaces the KV-block loop with an ancestor walk of exactly
    $\depth + 1$ iterations, achieving $\bigO{\numtokens \cdot \depth}$
    complexity per head.  Unlike DeFT, it requires no auxiliary index
    structures---ancestor positions are computed analytically from the
    BFS node index.
  \item We provide \textbf{autotuned configurations} for two NVIDIA
    microarchitectures (SM75 Turing and SM90 Hopper) with
    Hopper-specific optimizations: large Q-tiles exploiting
    shared memory, \texttt{int64} pointer arithmetic for $>$2\,GB tensors,
    and dtype-aware BF16 stores.
  \item We benchmark against \textbf{all three SOTA baselines} on
    the NVIDIA H100 SXM (Hopper):
    FlashAttention-2 (via PyTorch SDPA flash backend),
    FlashInfer~\cite{ye2025flashinfer}, and
    DeFT~\cite{yao2024deft}.
    \method{} achieves \textbf{up to 49$\times$} over FA-2,
    \textbf{101$\times$} over FlashInfer, and
    \textbf{109$\times$} over DeFT at large tree sizes.
    At practical EAGLE-class configurations
    ($\branching{=}3, \depth{=}7, \batchsize{=}8$), speedups are
    \textbf{11.9$\times$}, \textbf{22.3$\times$}, and
    \textbf{45.6$\times$} respectively.
  \item We characterize the \textbf{quadratic padding waste} incurred by
    dense attention on tree-shaped batches, showing it exceeds
    $99\%$ for moderate tree sizes, and present an
    \textbf{Amdahl's-law E2E projection} estimating verification-pass
    speedup in a full transformer stack.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Speculative Decoding}

Speculative decoding \cite{leviathan2023fast, chen2023accelerating, stern2018blockwise}
decomposes autoregressive generation into a \emph{draft} phase and a
\emph{verify} phase.
A lightweight draft model generates $\gamma$ candidate tokens, and the
target model evaluates them in a single batched forward pass.
Accepted tokens are committed; the first rejection triggers resampling.
The expected wall-clock speedup is $\mathbb{E}[\tau] / c_{\text{verify}}$,
where $\tau$ is the acceptance length and $c_{\text{verify}}$ is the
target-model latency per verification step.

\subsection{Tree-Structured Drafts}

EAGLE-2 \cite{li2024eagle2}, Medusa \cite{cai2024medusa}, and
EAGLE-3 \cite{li2025eagle3} grow a tree of $\numtokens$ candidate
tokens rather than a single chain, organized as a complete
$\branching$-ary tree of depth $\depth$.
EAGLE-3 uses trees with up to 60 draft tokens at depth~8;
EAGLE-2 typically uses depth~6.
Nodes are BFS-ordered (level-order), so root~$= 0$ and
$\parent(k) = \lfloor (k - 1) / \branching \rfloor$ for $k \geq 1$.

\paragraph{Attention rule.}
During verification, the attention mask is defined by the tree
structure: token $i$ may attend to token $j$ if and only if
$j$ is an ancestor of $i$ (including self-attention at $i = j$).
For a node at depth $\ell$, this yields exactly $\ell + 1$
non-zero entries in the $N$-wide mask row.

\subsection{Dense Attention Is Wasteful on Trees}

Standard scaled dot-product attention computes the full
$\numtokens \times \numtokens$ attention matrix and applies a mask:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \softmax\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{\headdim}}
    \odot M\right) V,
\end{equation}
where $M \in \{0, -\infty\}^{\numtokens \times \numtokens}$ is the
tree-ancestry mask.
Even optimized IO-aware kernels such as FlashAttention-2 stream all
$\numtokens$ KV blocks per query, performing
$\bigO{\numtokens^2 \headdim}$ FLOPs.
For $\branching = 4$, $\depth = 5$:
\[
  \frac{\text{useful FMAs}}{\text{total FMAs}}
  = \frac{(\depth + 1)}{\numtokens}
  = \frac{6}{1{,}365} = 0.44\%.
\]

\paragraph{Padding waste.}
When sequences of varying lengths within a speculative-decoding batch
are padded to the maximum length $L_{\max}$, the quadratic
attention-compute waste is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:padding-waste}
  r_{\text{attn}} = \frac{\batchsize \cdot L_{\max}^2 - \sum_{i=1}^{\batchsize} L_i^2}
  {\batchsize \cdot L_{\max}^2}.
\end{equation}
When most sequences are short drafts but one is the full prompt +
tree, $r_{\text{attn}}$ approaches $1 - 1/\batchsize$, wasting
nearly all compute in the batch.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Ancestor-Sparse Flash Attention}

The key insight is that for BFS-ordered trees, the
ancestor positions of any query token can be computed arithmetically
without materializing the full attention mask.
Given query position $q$ in sequence-local coordinates, its
$s$-th ancestor is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ancestor-walk}
  a^{(0)}(q) = q, \quad
  a^{(s)}(q) = \left\lfloor \frac{a^{(s-1)}(q) - 1}{\branching} \right\rfloor
  \quad \text{for } s \geq 1,
\end{equation}
converging to the root ($a^{(s)} = 0$) for $s \geq \depth_q$.

\paragraph{Kernel structure.}
\method{} replaces the standard KV-block loop of FlashAttention-2
with an \emph{ancestor walk loop} of exactly $\depth + 1$ iterations.
At each step~$s$, for every query row $q_m$ in a BLOCK\_M-wide Q-tile:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Compute ancestor position:}
    $\text{cur}_m = a^{(s)}(q_{\text{off}} + m)$ via \Cref{eq:ancestor-walk}.
  \item \textbf{Skip duplicates:} If $\text{cur}_m = \text{prev}_m$
    (the root revisited after all ancestors are exhausted), skip the update.
  \item \textbf{Scattered gather:}
    Load $k_m = K[\text{seq\_start} + \text{cur}_m, h, :]$ and
    $v_m = V[\text{seq\_start} + \text{cur}_m, h, :]$.
  \item \textbf{Flash-Attention-2 online softmax update:}
    Compute $\text{score}_m = \langle q_m, k_m \rangle / \sqrt{\headdim}$,
    then update running max $m_i$, normalization $\ell_i$, and
    accumulator using the standard two-pass rescaling
    \cite{milakov2018online}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Complexity.}
Each Q-tile processes $\text{BLOCK\_M}$ queries, each requiring
$\depth + 1$ scattered K/V gathers and dot products of dimension
$\headdim$.
The per-head complexity is:
\begin{equation}
  \bigO{\frac{\numtokens}{\text{BLOCK\_M}} \cdot \text{BLOCK\_M} \cdot (\depth + 1) \cdot \headdim}
  = \bigO{\numtokens \cdot \depth \cdot \headdim},
\end{equation}
compared to $\bigO{\numtokens^2 \cdot \headdim}$ for dense kernels.
For $\branching = 4$, $\depth = 5$: the reduction factor is
$\numtokens / (\depth + 1) = 1{,}365 / 6 \approx 227\times$.

\subsection{Ragged Batching}

To avoid the quadratic padding waste of \Cref{eq:padding-waste},
\method{} operates on a \emph{ragged} (packed) tensor layout.
All $\batchsize$ sequences are concatenated into a single 1-D token
dimension, indexed by a \texttt{seq\_starts} array of offsets.
Each CTA reads its batch index from a precomputed mapping and
uses \texttt{seq\_starts} to resolve global token positions.

This eliminates both token-level padding (filling short sequences
to $L_{\max}$) and attention-compute padding (the full
$\batchsize \cdot L_{\max}^2$ attention matrix).

\paragraph{Hopper-specific optimizations.}
On SM~9.0 (Hopper), three key adaptations improve performance:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Single pipeline stage:}
    The ancestor walk has data-dependent memory addresses
    ($a^{(s)}$ depends on $a^{(s-1)}$), preventing Triton's
    software pipelining (\texttt{num\_stages} $> 1$) from
    prefetching useful data.  Setting \texttt{num\_stages}$= 1$
    avoids wasted prefetch bandwidth.

  \item \textbf{int64 pointer offsets:}
    At $\batchsize \geq 64$, $\branching = 4$, $\depth = 8$, the
    total token count per batch reaches $\sim$5.6M.
    With $\headdim = 64$ and FP16 storage, byte offsets exceed
    $2^{31} - 1$ (= 2\,GB), causing silent wrap-around in int32
    pointer arithmetic.
    We cast all global pointer offsets to \texttt{tl.int64}.

  \item \textbf{Large Q-tiles:}
    Hopper's 228\,KB shared memory per SM allows BLOCK\_M
    up to 512 without spilling to global memory, compared to
    BLOCK\_M $\leq 128$ on Turing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Hardware.}
We evaluate on two GPUs spanning three generations:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{NVIDIA T4} (Turing, SM~7.5): 16\,GB GDDR6,
    2{,}560 CUDA cores, 65\,TFLOPS FP16.
  \item \textbf{NVIDIA H100 SXM} (Hopper, SM~9.0): 80\,GB HBM3,
    132 SMs, 228\,KB shared memory/SM, 989\,TFLOPS FP16.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Software.}
PyTorch~2.8.0+cu121, Triton~3.x, Python~3.12 (H100);
PyTorch~2.1+cu118, Triton~2.1 (T4).

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare against three state-of-the-art attention kernels, all
running on the H100:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{FlashAttention-2} \cite{dao2023flashattention2}
    (via PyTorch SDPA flash backend):
    PyTorch's \texttt{scaled\_dot\_product\_attention} with
    \texttt{enable\_flash=True} dispatches to the native
    FlashAttention-2 kernel on SM~9.0 without requiring the
    separate \texttt{flash-attn} package.
    We apply the tree-ancestor mask via SDPA's mask interface.
    This is our \emph{primary comparison target}---it is the
    fastest dense-attention baseline.
  \item \textbf{FlashInfer} \cite{ye2025flashinfer}
    (MLSys~2025):
    We use FlashInfer's \texttt{single\_prefill\_with\_kv\_cache}
    with the tree-ancestor mask in ragged layout, dispatched
    per-item.  Pre-compiled SM90 cubins from the
    \texttt{flashinfer-cubin} package provide JIT-free launch.
  \item \textbf{DeFT} \cite{yao2024deft} (ICLR~2025):
    We use the standalone Triton implementation from
    PanZaifeng/FastTree-Artifact, which is compatible with our
    PyTorch~2.8.0 environment.
    DeFT partitions tree nodes by shared KV prefixes and compresses
    the tree mask into a 64-bit binary chain mask (BCM).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Scope of kernel benchmarks.}
All kernel timings measure the attention operator alone—\emph{not} a
full model forward pass.
The tree attention processes the \emph{intra-tree} Q/K/V block only:
it does not include the prefix context-KV-cache attention that occurs
in a real verification pass.
Context-prefix attention is dense and can reuse any standard kernel
(FlashAttention-2, FlashDecoding, etc.); our contribution is the
sparse intra-tree component.

\paragraph{Configurations.}
We sweep branching factor $\branching \in \{2, 3, 4\}$,
tree depth $\depth \in \{1, \ldots, 8\}$,
batch size $\batchsize \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$,
head dimension $\headdim = 64$, and $\nheads = 8$ attention heads.
All timings are the median of 50 iterations after 10 warmup rounds,
measured with \texttt{torch.cuda.Event} elapsed time.

\paragraph{Limitation: head dimension.}
We benchmark at $\headdim = 64$, not $\headdim = 128$ as used by
LLaMA-2/3 and Mistral.
The ancestor-walk algorithm is agnostic to $\headdim$; larger heads
increase the per-gather dot-product cost equally for all kernels.
We expect speedup ratios to be similar at $\headdim = 128$ but
acknowledge this as an untested assumption.

\subsection{Latency vs.\ Tree Depth}

\section{End-to-End Projection}\label{sec:e2e}
% =====================================================================

A kernel-level speedup translates to end-to-end gains only to the
extent that attention dominates total verification-pass latency.
We estimate this via Amdahl's law.

\paragraph{Attention fraction in practice.}
In a synthetic LLaMA-2-7B stack (32 layers, $\headdim = 128$,
$H = 32$, SwiGLU FFN with $d_{\text{ffn}} = 11{,}008$), we measure
the attention fraction using our ragged kernel at various tree sizes.
At $\batchsize = 8$, $\branching = 3$:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item $\depth = 5$ ($N = 364$): attention accounts for
    $\approx 34\%$ of total forward time.
  \item $\depth = 7$ ($N = 3{,}280$): attention accounts for
    $\approx 34\%$ of total forward time.
\end{itemize}
These fractions are modest because the linear projections
(QKV, output, gate/up/down) dominate at moderate token counts.

\paragraph{Amdahl projection.}
Let $\alpha$ denote the attention fraction under the ragged kernel.
If \method{} provides a $k\times$ kernel speedup over FA-2,
the maximum end-to-end speedup is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:amdahl}
  S_{\text{e2e}} = \frac{1}{(1 - \alpha) + \alpha / k}
  \;\leq\; \frac{1}{1 - \alpha}.
\end{equation}
For our measured $\alpha \approx 0.34$ and $k = 11.9\times$
($\branching = 3$, $\depth = 7$, $\batchsize = 8$), this yields:
\[
  S_{\text{e2e}} \approx \frac{1}{0.66 + 0.34/11.9}
  = \frac{1}{0.689}
  \approx 1.45\times.
\]
That is, an 11.9$\times$ \emph{kernel} speedup over FA-2 translates
to approximately \textbf{1.45$\times$ verification-pass} speedup in a
full transformer.
At $\batchsize = 128$, $\branching = 4$, $\depth = 6$ where the
kernel speedup reaches 49$\times$, the projection is:
\[
  S_{\text{e2e}} \approx \frac{1}{0.66 + 0.34/49.1}
  \approx 1.49\times.
\]
The E2E impact grows with tree depth and batch size, as the $O(N^2)$
attention cost eventually dominates the linear projections.

\paragraph{Honest assessment.}
We present these projections rather than synthetic whole-model numbers
because our current E2E benchmark uses random weights (not real
LLaMA-2 parameters) and measures only our kernel---not a full
speculative decoding pipeline with a draft model.
A complete E2E evaluation with real model weights, EAGLE-2/3 draft
heads, and standard benchmarks (MT-bench, Spec-Bench) is future work.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Speculative decoding.}
The foundational framework was proposed independently by
\citet{leviathan2023fast} and \citet{chen2023accelerating}.
Tree-structured extensions include EAGLE-2 \cite{li2024eagle2},
EAGLE-3 \cite{li2025eagle3}, Medusa \cite{cai2024medusa},
SpecInfer \cite{miao2024specinfer}, Sequoia \cite{chen2024sequoia},
and Hydra \cite{ankner2024hydra}.
EAGLE-3 is the current state of the art, reaching 4.4--6.5$\times$
speedup by fusing draft-head training into pre-training;
it uses depth-8 trees with 60 draft tokens and integrates with
SGLang and vLLM serving frameworks~\cite{li2025eagle3}.
These works focus on draft quality and acceptance rate; our
contribution is orthogonal, optimizing the attention kernel in the
verification pass.

\paragraph{Tree-aware attention kernels.}
DeFT \cite{yao2024deft} (ICLR~2025) is the closest prior work.
It partitions tree nodes into KV-guided groups sharing common
ancestor prefixes, reorders them depth-first for coalesced memory
access, and compresses the tree mask into a 64-bit binary chain
mask (BCM).
DeFT reports 3.59$\times$ attention speedup over FlashAttention-2 on
A100 and 2.23$\times$ end-to-end speedup on LLaMA-3.1-Instruct-8B.
Our approach differs in three ways: (1) we use Q-guided ancestor walks
rather than KV-guided grouping, requiring no auxiliary data structures;
(2) our kernel natively supports ragged (packed) batching, eliminating
padding waste entirely; (3) our Triton implementation is
architecture-portable, running on both Turing~SM75 and Hopper~SM90.
In our direct comparison on the H100 (\Cref{tab:results}), \method{}
outperforms DeFT by up to \textbf{109$\times$} at large tree sizes,
though DeFT supports $\headdim = 128$ and GQA natively.

SpecInfer \cite{miao2024specinfer} encodes tree topology in a 64-bit
bitmask within its TopK token tree verifier, limiting tree size to
64~tokens.
Our approach has no such limit.

\paragraph{Efficient attention.}
FlashAttention \cite{dao2022flashattention} and FlashAttention-2
\cite{dao2023flashattention2} introduced IO-aware tiling for dense
attention, reducing HBM traffic from $\bigO{N^2}$ to $\bigO{N}$ at
the cost of recomputation.
FlashInfer \cite{ye2025flashinfer} (MLSys~2025) provides a
composable attention engine with block-sparse row (BSR) format,
JIT-compiled CUDA kernels, and CUDAGraph-compatible planning.
It serves as the attention backend in SGLang and achieves 1.38$\times$
throughput improvement with EAGLE-3 at batch~64~\cite{li2025eagle3}.
FlashInfer does not exploit tree-specific sparsity---it processes all
KV blocks regardless of ancestry.
In our benchmarks (\Cref{tab:results}), \method{} outperforms
FlashInfer by up to \textbf{101$\times$} on the H100.
PagedAttention \cite{kwon2023vllm} optimizes KV cache memory
management for serving.
Ring Attention \cite{liu2023ring} distributes attention across devices.
None of these exploit the structure-specific sparsity of tree attention.

\paragraph{Sparse attention.}
Block-sparse attention \cite{child2019generating},
BigBird \cite{zaheer2020bigbird}, and Longformer \cite{beltagy2020longformer}
target regular sparsity patterns (local windows + global tokens).
Tree attention patterns are irregular—each row has a different set
of ancestors—making block-sparse approaches inapplicable.
Our kernel handles this irregularity via per-row scattered gathers.

\paragraph{Triton GPU programming.}
Triton \cite{tillet2019triton} provides a Python-embedded DSL for
writing GPU kernels at a level of abstraction above CUDA.
Our use of Triton's \texttt{@triton.autotune} infrastructure enables
portable performance across two GPU generations (Turing and Hopper)
without hand-tuned CUDA or CUTLASS templates.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{When is \method{} most beneficial?}
The speedup increases with tree size ($\numtokens$) because the
$\bigO{N^2}$ cost of dense attention grows quadratically while
\method{}'s cost grows as $\bigO{N \cdot \depth}$.
For very small trees ($\numtokens < 500$), the overhead of ragged
indexing and scattered gathers exceeds the savings from reduced work,
and FlashAttention-2 remains faster
(\Cref{tab:results}: speedup $\approx 0.5\text{--}0.7\times$ at small $N$).
The crossover to $>1\times$ occurs around $\numtokens \approx 500\text{--}1{,}000$
depending on branching factor and batch size.
In practice, EAGLE-2 uses depth~6 and EAGLE-3 uses depth~8, placing
typical verification passes firmly in \method{}'s advantage region.

\paragraph{Integration with serving frameworks.}
\method{} is designed as a drop-in replacement for SDPA in the
verification pass.  It accepts packed (ragged) Q/K/V tensors and
a \texttt{seq\_starts} offset array, matching the interface expected
by vLLM \cite{kwon2023vllm} and TensorRT-LLM's ragged batching.
In a full speculative decoding pipeline, \method{} would handle
only the intra-tree attention block; the context-prefix KV cache
attention would continue to use FlashAttention-2 or FlashDecoding.

\paragraph{Comparison with DeFT.}
DeFT~\cite{yao2024deft} reports 3.59$\times$ attention speedup over
FA-2 on A100; in our direct H100 comparison, \method{} achieves up to
49$\times$ over FA-2 and 109$\times$ over DeFT itself
(\Cref{tab:results}).
The key difference is algorithmic: DeFT groups queries by shared KV
prefixes (KV-guided), while \method{} walks ancestors per-query
(Q-guided).  DeFT's approach achieves better memory coalescing for the
KV reads but still performs $O(N)$ work per group; our ancestor walk
performs exactly $\depth + 1$ gathers per query regardless of $N$.
DeFT's production implementation also supports $\headdim = 128$ and
GQA, which we do not yet support.
Conceptually, the Q-guided and KV-guided approaches may be
complementary.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
We identify four limitations of the current work:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Head dimension.}
    We benchmark at $\headdim = 64$ with $\nheads = 8$.
    Production LLMs (LLaMA-2/3, Mistral) use $\headdim = 128$ with
    $\nheads = 32$.
    While the ancestor-walk algorithm is $\headdim$-agnostic, we have
    not verified that speedup ratios hold at $\headdim = 128$.
  \item \textbf{No grouped-query attention (GQA).}
    Modern models use GQA (e.g., 8 KV heads, 32 Q heads).
    Our kernel assumes MHA ($\nheads_K = \nheads_Q$).
    Extension to GQA requires sharing K/V gathers across Q-head groups
    but no algorithmic changes.
  \item \textbf{Intra-tree only.}
    Real verification passes compute
    $\text{Attn}(q_{\text{tree}}, [K_{\text{ctx}} ; K_{\text{tree}}],
    [V_{\text{ctx}} ; V_{\text{tree}}])$.
    Our kernel handles only the $K_{\text{tree}} / V_{\text{tree}}$
    block.
    The context prefix uses standard flash attention and is unaffected
    by tree sparsity.
    At large context lengths, the prefix dominates and our kernel's
    contribution to E2E latency shrinks.
  \item \textbf{Synthetic weights.}
    All E2E benchmarks use random fp16 weights rather than real
    LLaMA-2/3 parameters.
    Timing trends (attention fraction, scaling behavior) should
    generalize, but absolute throughput numbers are synthetic.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================

We presented \method{}, an ancestor-sparse Flash Attention kernel
for tree-structured speculative decoding verification.
By replacing the $\bigO{N^2}$ KV-block loop with an
$\bigO{N \cdot \depth}$ ancestor walk derived from BFS tree structure,
\method{} achieves up to \textbf{49$\times$} kernel-level speedup
over FlashAttention-2, \textbf{101$\times$} over FlashInfer, and
\textbf{109$\times$} over DeFT on the NVIDIA H100, scaling from
batch~1 to batch~128.
At practical EAGLE-class tree sizes
($\branching = 3$, $\depth = 7$, $\batchsize = 8$), speedups are
11.9$\times$, 22.3$\times$, and 45.6$\times$ respectively.
Via Amdahl's law, this projects to $\sim$1.45$\times$ verification-pass
speedup in a LLaMA-2-7B-class model---modest but meaningful, and
growing with tree depth.

The kernel's advantage increases with tree size, aligning with the
trend toward deeper draft trees in EAGLE-3 \cite{li2025eagle3}
and Sequoia \cite{chen2024sequoia}.
As tree-structured speculative decoding becomes the dominant inference
paradigm, the intra-tree attention bottleneck will grow, and
structure-aware kernels like \method{} will become a necessary
building block.

\paragraph{Future work.}
(1)~Extend to $\headdim = 128$ and GQA for LLaMA-3 compatibility.
(2)~Integrate into SGLang/vLLM and measure end-to-end speedup
with EAGLE-2/3 draft heads on standard benchmarks (MT-bench,
Spec-Bench).
(3)~Handle context-prefix + tree attention in a single fused kernel.
(4)~Extend to Blackwell SM~12.0 when baseline libraries add support.
